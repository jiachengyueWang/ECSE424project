Design Evolution

Our prototype has been rebuilt to a better standard based on the feedback from the evaluation team and test subjects. Their suggestions provide a good opportunity for us to make any improvements. We took some time to think and analyze, the following list is the design evolution for our next prototype iteration, the Alpha system:


  • 1. Unnecessary operations are required during the process:

    • It is pointed out by our evaluation team that test subjects often complained about the problem with unnecessary clicks, which caused confusion. For example, notifications including “You’re in the queue” and “You’re next” require clicks to disappear, which really made our prototype ineffective. And we tried many existing applications, notification pop-ups would stay normally no more than 3 seconds. Thus, it is a good point that we need to keep focus on.

    In addition, according to the heuristic Aesthetic and minimalist design in the Nielson’s heuristics list, interfaces should not contain information which is irrelevant or rarely needed. Our further modification to this usability issue is that a new red cross icon will be on the top right side of the pop-up window indicating to close the window, users can immediately recognize it as a notification and close it any time they would like to.

  • 2. The dequeue button is too small and next to the user’s name too close:

    • The issue is also brought up by the evaluation team. During their testing, when users want to click the red cross button to dequeue, they may accidentally click their names(which puts them first in the queue). Then an unexpected and strange interface would be shown to the user. To avoid this, we decided to enlarge the red cross button and make the gap between the button and user’s name unclickable, which may prevent misclicking from happening.

    The issue is related to the heuristic Consistency and standards in Nielsen's list. The previous design did not provide a consistent user interface. We implemented two clickable buttons being too close to each other at first. With the improvement, users should have a better control of the system(less misclicking).

  • 3. The EasyAsk button will turn green when the functionality is activated to display images:

    • The modification was finalized when we were having team discussions. We used to think on the side of someone who is currently using the feature, but it is clearly not enough. Let’s imagine you are in the middle of a meeting and you have not clicked the EasyAsk button, and suddenly a shared image displays in front you without any notifications and the presenter and some participants are talking about a question that you are not familiar with, which really does not keep the users informed about what is going on.

    The issue is related to the Heuristic Visibility of system status in Niesen’s list. Our solution to this is to make the EasyAsk green when someone is currently using the feature. By doing so, for those participants who have clicked the button can see an indicator of what will happen in a minute(a pop-up window will show up, presenter and some participants will start a discussion on the basis of this image).

  • 4. Users are confused with the queue.

    • As a fact that it is not a perfect prototype, there is insufficient information describing the use of the queue. When users choose to make a new meeting and click the EasyAsk button, it is actually for the presenter not the participants. Clicking the names in the queue is saying this person has been granted permission from a presenter’s perspective since at the current stage, we could not have two people or more using our prototype simultaneously. Then, after clicking the EasyAsk button the second time, users can go into “participant mode” and see what they are supposed to see and operate as participants. To avoid this confusion, We will add some notifications saying the current step(interface) is for the presenter.


Prototype Revisions

We modified our design compared to the computer prototype. By focusing on feedback and being realistic, we minimized unnecessary interactions and reduced on-click feedback time for the system. We decided to reevaluate the feasibility and development effort necessary to make the EASYASK tool functional as an inbuilt tool in Zoom.
